Polyphonic Piano
& Sequencer

Analog synthesis · Discrete components · Audio signal processing

Overview

How does this work?

The Polyphonic Piano uses a series of 556 timers in an astable multivibrator circuit to produce frequencies matching 6 notes in the C5 octave. Each note's frequency is set by a 500kΩ potentiometer. All 6 note signals are fed into an LM741 summing amplifier, which combines them into a single signal. That signal is then directed to an LM386 audio amplifier and speaker circuit, which converts it into sound.

The sequencer is built from an LM555 timer and a 4017 IC. The 555 controls how often the notes play; the 4017 sequences them in order — producing a melody.

Circuit Architecture

Signal chain.

Stage 1
Oscillator Bank — 556 Timers (×6)

Each 556 timer runs in astable multivibrator mode, producing a square wave at the frequency of one note (C, D, E, F, G, A in the C5 octave). Frequency is tuned via a 500kΩ potentiometer per note.

Stage 2
Summing Amplifier — LM741

All six oscillator outputs feed into an inverting summing amplifier. The LM741 combines their voltages into a single mixed signal for downstream amplification.

Stage 3
Audio Output — LM386 & Speaker

The summed signal is amplified by the LM386 low-voltage audio power amplifier, which drives the speaker to produce audible sound.

Stage 4
Sequencer — LM555 + 4017 IC

The LM555 timer controls the clock frequency — determining how fast the sequence plays. The 4017 decade counter steps through each note in order, producing a repeating melody.

Context

How is this different from an electric piano?

Electric pianos use microprocessors to reproduce realistic acoustic tones through digital signal processing. This circuit produces musical notes entirely through square-wave oscillation using discrete analog components — no microcontrollers, no digital processing, no moving parts. Polyphony is physically constrained by the number of oscillators on the breadboard rather than computational limits.

Reflections

What I learned.

How it evolved

The build started as a basic circuit with 6 LM556 timers producing different waveforms, then became a digital piano capable of producing 6 in-tune notes, and finally evolved into a fully functioning system — summing signals, amplifying the waveform, controlling tempo, sequencing notes, and producing audio output.

What went well and what didn't

  • Calculations for unknown component values (capacitors, resistors, potentiometers) were accurate
  • Wiring was longer than needed and the breadboard layout wasn't planned in advance — made troubleshooting difficult
  • Parts list was created after the build rather than before, requiring components to be removed to identify values
  • Over 5 hours of troubleshooting required to resolve wiring and organization issues

Next time

Plan the breadboard layout before building, keep a running log of calculated component values, and track the parts list throughout the build — not after. Better organization upfront would significantly cut build and debug time.
