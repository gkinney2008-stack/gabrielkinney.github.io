🏎️ Mousetrap Car Design

Mechanical Engineering Project | EE4000 | 2024

← Back to Portfolio

Project Overview

Designed and optimized a mousetrap-powered race car achieving 364% improvement in distance over the baseline design. The project involved engineering analysis of mechanical advantage, energy transfer, friction reduction, and structural optimization.

Key Achievement: Increased vehicle distance from baseline 1 meter to 3.64 meters through systematic optimization of wheel size, lever arm, and friction characteristics. Demonstrated mastery of mechanical engineering principles including torque, mechanical advantage, and energy conservation.

Project Metrics

Baseline Performance

1.0 meter distance

Final Performance

3.64 meters distance

Improvement

364% increase

Key Variable

Mechanical advantage optimization

Design Approach

1. Energy Analysis

Analyzed the mousetrap spring energy storage and calculated theoretical maximum energy available. Used principles of elastic potential energy (PE = ½kx²) to determine baseline energy budget.

2. Mechanical Advantage

Optimized the lever arm ratio to maximize torque at the axle. Extended trap arm length while maintaining spring compression ratio to increase rotational force on wheels.

3. Wheel Optimization

Increased wheel diameter from baseline 2 inches to optimized 4+ inches. Larger wheels:

  • Increase linear velocity from rotational motion
  • Reduce ground friction (fewer rotations for same distance)
  • Improve traction with ground contact

4. Friction Reduction

Minimized friction losses through:

  • Precision wheel bearings
  • Lubricated axles and joints
  • Lightweight structure to reduce rolling resistance
  • Wheel material optimization for low friction coefficient

Technical Design Details

Chassis Material

Balsa wood and aluminum for optimal strength-to-weight ratio

Wheel Configuration

4-inch diameter wheels with precision bearings and minimal runout

Drive System

Extended lever arm with timing-optimized rope release mechanism

Axle Design

1/4" steel axles with plastic sleeve bearings and lubrication

Weight Optimization

Total car mass: ~200g (minimal structure, maximum efficiency)

Alignment

Precision wheel alignment and load distribution to minimize friction

Mathematical Analysis

Mechanical Advantage Calculation

Formula: Mechanical Advantage = Effort Arm / Load Arm

  • Baseline trap arm (effort arm): 4 inches
  • Load arm (distance to wheel axle): 2 inches
  • Baseline MA: 4/2 = 2.0
  • Optimized effort arm: 6 inches
  • Optimized MA: 6/2 = 3.0
  • Torque increase: 50% from mechanical advantage alone

Wheel Effect on Velocity

Linear Velocity: v = ω × r (angular velocity × radius)

  • Baseline wheel radius: 1 inch
  • Optimized wheel radius: 2 inches
  • Velocity increase: 2× from doubled wheel radius
  • Combined with mechanical advantage: ~3× total distance increase potential

Testing & Validation

  • Multiple runs: 5+ test runs per design iteration
  • Distance measurement: Marked floor with tape, measured to vehicle's final position
  • Consistency testing: Verified repeatability of results (±5% variance)
  • Component analysis: Tested individual modifications (wheels, arm length, friction) in isolation
  • Failure mode analysis: Identified mechanical failures and redesigned accordingly

Design Iterations

Iteration 1: Baseline

Distance: 1.0 meter - Served as benchmark for comparison

Iteration 2: Mechanical Advantage

Extended arm to 5 inches, increased distance to 1.8 meters (80% improvement)

Iteration 3: Larger Wheels

Increased wheel diameter to 3 inches, distance increased to 2.5 meters (150% improvement)

Iteration 4: Friction Reduction

Added precision bearings and lubrication, distance increased to 3.2 meters (220% improvement)

Iteration 5: Optimization

Final tuning of all variables, achieved 3.64 meters (364% improvement)

Broader Applications

The optimization principles learned apply to many engineering fields:

  • Vehicle Design: Fuel efficiency through drag reduction and weight optimization

  • Manufacturing: Process optimization and tolerance management

Conclusion: This project demonstrated the power of systematic engineering analysis combined with iterative testing. Small improvements in mechanical advantage, wheel size, and friction reduction compounded to achieve over 3.6x improvement in performance—illustrating how fundamentals of mechanical engineering translate to real-world optimization challenges.
