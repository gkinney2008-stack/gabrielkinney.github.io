Mousetrap Car Design

Energy optimization · Mechanical advantage · Iterative testing

Overview

Project summary.

Designed and optimized a mousetrap-powered race car through systematic engineering analysis of mechanical advantage, energy transfer, friction reduction, and structural optimization. Each design iteration was informed by quantitative testing and failure mode analysis.

1.0 m Baseline distance
1.1 m Final distance
10% Improvement over baseline
5+ Runs per iteration
Design Approach

Engineering methodology.

Energy Analysis

Analyzed mousetrap spring energy storage and calculated theoretical maximum energy using elastic potential energy principles (PE = ½kx²) to establish the baseline energy budget.

Mechanical Advantage

Optimized the lever arm ratio to maximize torque at the axle. Extended trap arm length while maintaining spring compression ratio to increase rotational force transferred to the wheels.

Wheel Optimization

  • Increased wheel diameter to raise linear velocity from rotational motion
  • Reduced ground friction through fewer rotations per unit distance
  • Improved traction via optimized ground contact geometry

Friction Reduction

  • Precision wheel bearings at all axle contact points
  • Lubricated axles and joints to minimize drag losses
  • Lightweight structure to reduce rolling resistance
  • Wheel material selected for low friction coefficient
Technical Details

Design specifications.

Chassis Material Balsa wood and aluminum — optimal strength-to-weight ratio
Wheel Configuration 4-inch diameter with precision bearings and minimal runout
Drive System Extended lever arm with timing-optimized rope release mechanism
Axle Design ¼″ steel axles with plastic sleeve bearings and lubrication
Total Weight ~200g — minimal structure, maximum efficiency
Alignment Precision wheel alignment and load distribution to minimize friction
Analysis

Mathematical breakdown.

Mechanical Advantage — MA = Effort Arm / Load Arm

  • Baseline effort arm: 4 in · Load arm: 2 in · MA = 2.0
  • Optimized effort arm: 6 in · Optimized MA = 3.0
  • Torque increase from mechanical advantage alone: 50%

Wheel Velocity — v = ω × r

  • Baseline wheel radius: 1 in → Optimized: 2 in
  • Linear velocity increase: 2× from doubled radius
  • Combined with MA improvement: ~3× theoretical distance potential
Iteration Log

Design iterations.

Iteration 1
Baseline — 1.0 m

Reference design. Established benchmark for all subsequent comparisons.

Iteration 2
Mechanical Advantage — 1.03 m

Extended arm to 5 inches. Marginal gain; spring tension constraints limited torque transfer.

Iteration 3
Larger Wheels — 1.06 m

Increased wheel diameter to 3 inches. Improved rolling efficiency, reduced slippage.

Iteration 4
Friction Reduction — 1.08 m

Added precision bearings and lubrication. Reduced energy loss at axle contact points.

Iteration 5
Final Tuning — 1.1 m

Combined all optimizations. 10% total improvement over baseline with ±5% run variance.

Takeaways

Key outcomes.

  • Mechanical advantage and torque fundamentals applied in physical system
  • Energy conservation principles validated through iterative testing
  • Friction analysis and systematic minimization across subsystems
  • Iterative design methodology with quantitative comparison between runs
  • Component-level isolation testing — variables changed one at a time
  • Failure mode identification and redesign under real constraints

The optimization principles demonstrated here — leverage, friction reduction, and weight minimization — apply directly to vehicle drive systems, robotic actuators, and mechanical power transmission design.
